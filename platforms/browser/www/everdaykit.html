Finland is safe – so relax.... Congrats!
You have landed probably one of the safest places in the world.   
In Finland there are no dangerous animals, not even nasty bugs, except mosquitos but they are harmless.  
The streets are quite, hardly any pollution, food and water is clean.

Finns might enjoy silence but most of them really like to help foreigners in need.
And Finns talk surprisingly good English.  This advice goes of course everywhere you travel but it’s one of the most important ones:
ask for help.

Wear proper clothing!
Yep, it’s freezing out either wet Or both.
During winter the temperature might go low as -30C. The key is to wear clothes in many layers (in Finnish: ’kerrospukeutuminen’).
So wearing 3 pants on top of each other is just OK.
And remember to buy good beanie or woolly hat.

You can drink tap water.
The tap water is clean and 100% safe to drink. Don’t buy bottled water. It’s useless and also bad for nature.

In emergency, call "112".
112 is the emergency telephone number (in Finnish: ”yleinen hätänumero”) for police, ambulance and fire department (works everywhere in EU)
Don’t hesitate to call it!  More info (pdf)

Police is your friend.
Don’t be afraid to contact police in Finland. They are very trustworthy and there is minimal corruption in police force.
So in case of accident or even small crime, it’s better to call the police.

Need a prepaid? Go to R-kioski.
R-Kioski is the local ’7-Eleven’ i.e. convenience store for all small stuff like snacks and softdrinks.  You can find them in every city.
For foreigner, this is usually the first stop where to buy local prepaid sim-card for mobile phone. You can buy also bus and train tickets here.

Take vitamin-D.
At this high north, you wont get enough sun for your body to produce oh so important vitamin D.
  Official recommendation is to take 10 micrograms per day, every day of the year.

Biggest thread for your life in Finland?
Boredom. That’s right.
This is because of the darkness and cold winters, lack of people and activities outside…. It might get boring.
Best way to fight against loneliness, boredom and cold dark winter, is to find a hobby and be active as possible.

Credit cards are commonly used even in very small purchases. ATM’s are getting rare and hard to find.

Need food?
Grocery stores are usually open everyday between 7-21. Some are open until 23.  So you shouldn’t have troubles in getting your shopping done.

Restaurants close usually at 22.00.
Many restaurants close their kitchen between 21-22. Some are closed on sundays.   Usually Finns go to restaurant to have dinner quite early, between 18-21.

You can buy alcohol only from governement run spirit-shops called ”Alko”.  Grosery stores can sell alcohol when it has alcohol 4.7% or under.

And don’t even think about taking your drink outside a bar. You are allowed to drink in only in side bar. But now in toilet or separate tobacco area.
