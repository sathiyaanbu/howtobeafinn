Stereotype of a Finn is silent introvert that’s keeping his head down in shame.

They got it all wrong.

Because of the harsh weather conditions, we don’t have the luxury to gather in cafes and parks and socialize.

But Finns can be open, friendly and very humorous.
They just need some more time
to get started and get to know you. Or booze.
That will change everything.

Finns do love their personal space.
In fact, they would rather stand while riding a bus than sit with another person close to them.

Finns place great value on words and they avoid ‘unnecessary’ small talk.
That doesn’t mean they are rude.

If you promise something to a Finn, you should do that with no excuse, or you will lose person's respect.

So mean what you say.

Even they may seem shy,
they actually like to talk and help foreigners.

Gender equality has always been important in Finland.
It’s the first country in the world to give women both the right to vote and stand for election.

Finns are law-abinding.
If the traffic light for pedestrians is red but there are no vehicles, Finns still wait for the green light.

Finns stick to rules and laws. They are the ’Apt pupils of EU’.
So if you don’t want to stick out, wait for the green light.

Finns dress super-casual. Wearing sneakers and jeans in work or about anywhere is OK.
Suit or fancy dress is for weddings and funerals.

The main color in clothing is black.

And in music.

when they use some color in their shell suits (tuulipuku), well…

Let’s say that Finns don’t get to be in cover of Vogue that often.

Finns are not afraid to take their clothes off.
Wether it’s in sauna, locker room or sometimes even the streets (nude run is called ”Viuhahdus”), Finns feel comfortable when naked.
